## Pillar Architecture

```
Living in the Future (the invitation)
    |
    +-- The Factory Model (how we built this future)
    +-- The Context Library (the infrastructure under it)
    +-- Human-AI Teaming (what it's like to live here)
```

Pillar 1 is the umbrella. Every piece of content from Pillars 2-4 can be framed as a deeper dive into the world Pillar 1 invites you to tour. This is not a hierarchy of importance — it is a framing relationship. Pillar 3 content about the context library is not less important than Pillar 1 content about the spatial interface. But it can always be introduced as: "here's the foundation that makes the world possible."

### Cross-Pillar Linking

Content that touches multiple pillars is encouraged, not avoided. A post about how an AI agent used the context library to produce spatial content touches Pillars 2, 3, and 4 simultaneously. The pillar assignment is about primary framing, not exclusive territory. When a piece touches multiple pillars, assign it to the one whose audience you most want to reach.

## Pillar 1: Living in the Future

### Core Position

We are not building toward a future — we live in it. Our projects are hex tiles on a spatial map. Our AI teammates have defined jobs and visible positions. Our weeks have structure and rhythm managed through systems we built. This is not a pitch. It is a daily reality, and you can come see it.

### Why This Pillar Exists

No other content creator in this space can show a traversable, inhabited future of work. Commentators can speculate. Researchers can study. We can give you a tour. That asymmetry is the entire communications strategy in one sentence.

### Cluster Topics

| # | Cluster | What It Covers | Content Examples |
|---|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **The Tour** | Walking someone through the spatial interface — Life Map, hex tiles, zoom levels, planning tools | Product demo video, screenshot walkthroughs, "here's what I see when I open my day" |
| 2 | **Before/After** | Concrete comparisons of spatial vs. list-based work. What changes when work has a place? | Side-by-side: Notion page vs. hex tile. "I used to track this in a spreadsheet, now I see it on my map" |
| 3 | **A Day in LifeBuild** | What a real day looks like. Morning planning session, mid-day adaptation, end-of-day review — all spatial | Narrative walkthroughs, time-lapse of a planning day, "here's how I decided what to work on today" |
| 4 | **The Factory Room** | The physical manifestation — TV screens with AI colleagues, real workstations, real work | Factory room video, behind-the-scenes setup, design decisions about physical-digital hybrid |
| 5 | **Moments of Recognition** | Specific instances where the spatial interface revealed something a list never would have | "I noticed two projects were adjacent on my map and realized they were the same problem." Short, punchy, specific |
| 6 | **The Visitor Experience** | What happens when someone sees LifeBuild for the first time. Their reactions, questions, confusions | Guest reaction posts, "the question everyone asks first," onboarding observations |

### Value Channels Served

- **Thought Leadership (primary):** This is the content that makes the right 50 think "these people understand what we're trying to become"
- **Internal Champions:** Target company employees see the spatial future and recognize the problem they're solving
- **Open Source Traction (secondary):** Tours drive curiosity about the infrastructure underneath

### Byline Authority

- **Jess (primary):** Spatial thinking, design judgment, visual authority. The tour guide.
- **Danvers (secondary):** Strategic framing, "why we built it this way," connecting spatial choices to organizational theory.

### Editorial Guidance

- Always show, never tell. Screenshots, video clips, spatial artifacts. If a post has no visual, it is probably a Pillar 4 post instead.
- Specificity over polish. A messy screenshot of a real planning session is more valuable than a designed mockup.
- The invitation frame: every piece should feel like "come see this" not "let me explain this."
- Crown jewel assets (product demo video, factory room video) live here. They are not content — they are strategic investments with dedicated appetite.

## Pillar 2: The Factory Model

### Core Position

A 2-person team can ship like a 10-person team when AI agents have defined jobs, a shared knowledge base, and a production pipeline. We are not using AI as a tool. We are running an AI-native operation — with agents who have roles, responsibilities, and accountability.

### Why This Pillar Exists

"Build in public" is not new. What is new is building in public while running an operation where most of the team is AI. The specificity of our factory — named agents, defined jobs, production stages, quality gates — is something no one else is documenting at this level. This is the pillar that makes engineering leaders lean forward.

### Cluster Topics

| # | Cluster | What It Covers | Content Examples |
|---|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **The Agent Roster** | Who the agents are, what jobs they do, how they're defined. Not anthropomorphized — role-defined | "Meet Sam, our librarian" — but framed as org design, not personality |
| 2 | **Production Pipeline** | Decide > Shape > Make > Patch. How content and code move through stages with appetite budgets and circuit breakers | Process walkthroughs, "how a feature goes from idea to shipped," pipeline bottleneck analysis |
| 3 | **The Betting Table** | How we decide what to build. Appetite, bets, cool-downs. Shape Up adapted for a 2-person AI-native team | Decision logs, "here's what we bet on this cycle and why," retrospective on bets that lost |
| 4 | **Metrics and Honest Numbers** | What the factory actually produces. Token usage, cycle times, output volume. Transparent, not boastful | "Our first factory run used X tokens and produced Y" — real numbers, real analysis |
| 5 | **Failures and Bottlenecks** | What breaks. Where the factory model falls apart. What we had to redesign | "This agent kept producing garbage until we..." — failure-first narratives |
| 6 | **Scaling Laws** | What happens to the factory as it grows. Adding agents, adding divisions, adding channels. Does it scale? | "We added a third agent and here's what changed," coordination cost analysis |

### Value Channels Served

- **Thought Leadership (primary):** Engineering leaders recognize sophisticated operational thinking
- **Internal Champions:** Target company employees see the methodology and think "we need this"
- **Open Source Traction (secondary):** Factory methodology is replicable — people want to build their own

### Byline Authority

- **Danvers (primary):** The strategist-builder. MBA applied to AI-native operations. Org design, process architecture.
- **Jess (secondary):** Production perspective, design pipeline integration, spatial production views.

### Editorial Guidance

- Lead with the system, not the tools. This is about operational architecture, not "look at this cool AI thing."
- Numbers are credibility. Include real metrics where possible. Token counts, cycle times, output volume. Transparency builds trust.
- The 37signals parallel is explicit: they documented how a small team builds calm software. We document how a small team builds with AI teammates. Same energy, different era.
- Avoid the "AI hype" frame. We are not excited about AI. We are running an operation. The tone is operational, not evangelical.

## Pillar 3: The Context Library

### Core Position

AI agents are only as good as the institutional knowledge they can access. The context library — a structured, auditable, version-controlled knowledge base designed for AI consumption — is the infrastructure that makes everything else work. It solves a problem every serious dev tools company faces and most haven't solved.

### Why This Pillar Exists

This is the primary pillar for open source traction. The context library is the asset most likely to be used by people outside LifeBuild, forked, starred, and contributed to. Every piece of content here drives people toward the GitHub repo. It is also the pillar most likely to create internal champions — engineers at target companies who recognize the problem and adopt the solution.

### Cluster Topics

| # | Cluster | What It Covers | Content Examples |
|---|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **The Problem** | Why AI agents fail without structured context. The "blank page" problem. Why RAG and embeddings alone don't solve it | "We gave an agent access to everything and it still couldn't do its job. Here's why." |
| 2 | **The Architecture** | How the library is structured. Card types, reference templates, provenance tracking, the audit trail | Technical deep dives, architecture diagrams, "here's how a card gets created and maintained" |
| 3 | **Usage Patterns** | How agents actually use the library. Query patterns, what they reach for, what they ignore | "Our librarian agent reads these cards 10x more than those. Here's what that tells us." |
| 4 | **Building Your Own** | How other teams can adopt the pattern. Getting started, templates, migration from existing docs | Guides, starter templates, "start here if you want to build a context library for your team" |
| 5 | **Quality and Decay** | How knowledge goes stale, how the audit system catches it, grading rubrics, the librarian's job | "We found 40% of our cards were outdated. Here's how we built the system that catches that." |
| 6 | **Case Studies** | Specific instances where the context library changed an outcome. An agent made a better decision because of a specific card | "This card saved us a week" — short, concrete, traceable |

### Value Channels Served

- **Open Source Traction (primary):** Stars, forks, external contributors, community adoption
- **Internal Champions (primary):** Engineers at target companies recognize the problem and use the solution
- **Thought Leadership (secondary):** Demonstrates infrastructure thinking at a level most teams haven't reached

### Byline Authority

- **Danvers (primary):** Architecture decisions, strategic rationale, the "why" of structured knowledge
- **Jess (secondary):** How spatial representation of knowledge differs from document-based, visual library concepts

### Editorial Guidance

- Technical depth is a feature, not a bug. This pillar's audience is engineers. They want specifics.
- Always link to the repo. Every piece of Pillar 3 content should include a path to the GitHub repository.
- The "building your own" cluster is the growth engine. Content that helps others adopt the pattern creates contributors who become community members who become internal champions.
- Avoid positioning the context library as proprietary advantage. It is open source. The advantage is that we built it first and use it most deeply.

## Pillar 4: Human-AI Teaming

### Core Position

Working alongside AI teammates is not what you expect. It is stranger, more nuanced, and more revealing than the hype suggests. We do it every day, and we document what it is actually like — the relationship dynamics, the judgment calls, the surprises, the failures, and the moments where the partnership produces something neither party could alone.

### Why This Pillar Exists

Everyone is talking about AI. Almost no one is documenting what it is actually like to work with AI agents as colleagues — not tools, not assistants, but teammates with defined roles. This pillar is the most personal, the most narrative-driven, and the most likely to generate emotional resonance. It connects to Pillar 1: the spatial interface is where teaming happens. You can see your agents on the map. The relationship is visible and traversable.

### Cluster Topics

| # | Cluster | What It Covers | Content Examples |
|---|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **The Relationship** | What it feels like to work with an AI colleague. Trust formation, delegation patterns, the uncanny valley of competence | "The moment I started trusting George with production decisions" — personal narrative |
| 2 | **Judgment Calls** | Where human judgment is irreplaceable. The approval gate. What agents should never decide alone | "Arvin drafted the strategy. Here's what I changed and why." — showing the human layer |
| 3 | **Surprises** | Things we did not expect. Emergent behaviors, unexpected agent strengths, blind spots we discovered | "We didn't design for this, but..." — discovery narratives |
| 4 | **Failures** | When teaming breaks down. Miscommunication, bad outputs, trust violations, the cost of over-delegation | "I let the agent handle it and here's what went wrong" — failure-first honesty |
| 5 | **The Visible Team** | What it means that teammates have positions on a spatial map. The experience of seeing your team, not just chatting with them | "I can see Conan working in the quality room. That changes something." — spatial teaming |
| 6 | **Role Design** | How you define an AI teammate's job. Not prompt engineering — organizational design for non-human colleagues | "Designing Arvin's job description: what we learned about AI role clarity" |

### Value Channels Served

- **Thought Leadership (primary):** Personal narratives from lived experience. The right 50 want to hear from people doing the work, not studying it.
- **Internal Champions:** Product leaders at target companies recognize the teaming patterns they want to build
- **Open Source Traction (tertiary):** Less direct, but the agent architecture that enables teaming is part of the open source offering

### Byline Authority

- **Danvers (primary):** The strategic and organizational perspective. Role design, delegation frameworks, the management theory of AI teaming.
- **Jess (primary):** The spatial and experiential perspective. What teaming feels like, looks like, sounds like in the spatial interface.

Both have equal authority here — different angles on the same lived experience.

### Editorial Guidance

- Personal narratives beat frameworks. This is the most human pillar. Lead with stories, not systems.
- Honesty is the differentiator. Everyone is selling AI success stories. We document failures, surprises, and ambiguity. That is what makes it credible.
- Connect to the spatial dimension. When possible, show that teaming happens in a place — not in a chat window. The visible team on the Life Map is the unique angle no one else has.
- Avoid anthropomorphizing for entertainment. Agents have roles, not personalities. The relationship is professional, not parasocial. Specificity about what an agent does is more compelling than giving it a backstory.

## Pillar Balance

Target distribution for the first quarter of content production:

| Pillar | Target Share | Rationale |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Living in the Future | 35% | The umbrella pillar. Highest differentiation. Every piece here reinforces the core position. |
| The Factory Model | 25% | Strong thought leadership. Appeals to engineering leaders. High specificity. |
| The Context Library | 20% | Open source traction driver. More technical, narrower audience, but highest conversion to GitHub. |
| Human-AI Teaming | 20% | Most personal and narrative-driven. Emotional resonance. Longer shelf life. |

This is a starting allocation, not a rule. Adjust based on what resonates. If Pillar 3 content drives disproportionate GitHub traction, shift allocation toward it. If Pillar 1 content generates engagement from the right 50, double down.

## Pillar Health Checks

During the weekly review, assess pillar health:

- **Coverage:** Has every pillar had at least one piece in the last two weeks?
- **Balance drift:** Is one pillar dominating because it's easier to produce, not because it's more valuable?
- **Cluster depth:** Are we hitting multiple clusters per pillar, or stuck on one angle?
- **Cross-pillar linking:** Are pieces connecting across pillars, or are pillars becoming silos?

If a pillar goes dark for more than two weeks, it is a signal — either the pillar is wrong (reconsider it) or production is avoiding it (investigate why).
