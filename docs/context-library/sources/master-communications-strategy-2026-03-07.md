## 1. Strategic Positioning

### Who We Are

Two founders — Danvers and Jess — building the future of human-AI collaboration. Not a marketing department. Not a content team. Two people with a thesis about how work changes when AI becomes a teammate, and a working prototype that proves it.

### What We Stand For

**Position-based authority:** We are not generalist AI commentators. We occupy a specific position: _the people who are living in the future of work, and you can come see it._ Every piece of content reinforces this position. We do not philosophize about what AI might become. We show you what it looks like to live and work alongside AI teammates, in a traversable world we built and inhabit daily.

This is the 37signals model applied to our situation. They occupied "calm company" and published from that position for twenty years. We occupy "living in the future of human-AI work" and publish from there.

The company is built on three independent bets — Spatial Visibility (work as a traversable landscape), Superior Process (structured frameworks for life), and AI as Teammates (agents with defined jobs). Our communications strategy does not explain these bets. It _shows_ them. The product is a traversable future. The factory is an AI-native office. Both can be toured — one digitally, one physically. That is the differentiator no one else can replicate.

### Voice

**Byline authority over brand voice.** Danvers and Jess post as themselves, never as "LifeBuild." Personal authority is the asset an acquirer buys. The company is the vehicle; the people are the product.

- Danvers: the strategist-builder. MBA who self-taught into AI-native product management. Inventing a job description that doesn't exist yet at most companies. Voice is direct, analytical, with enough warmth to feel human.
- Jess: the spatial thinker. Understands both the technical and spatial dimensions of human-AI collaboration. One of a small number of people thinking about this intersection. Voice is grounded, visual, specific.

Both voices share: conviction without arrogance, specificity over abstraction, documentation over proclamation.

## 2. The Acquihire Frame

Everything in this strategy exists to serve an acquihire outcome. Every recommendation, every resource allocation, every content decision should be traceable to one or more of the three value channels defined in the acquihire brief.

This is not a traditional marketing strategy. We are not building an audience. We are not generating leads. We are not growing a brand. We are creating the conditions under which the right people at the right companies encounter our work and think: _these are the people who understand what we are trying to become._

### Three Value Channels

| Channel                  | What It Means                                       | How Communications Serves It                                                                                                                                                                                |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Thought Leadership**   | The right 50 people see the work                    | Content that demonstrates lived expertise. LinkedIn, conferences, the factory demo video. Not audience size — audience precision.                                                                           |
| **Open Source Traction** | Context library gets stars and usage                | Documentation, community engagement, visibility in dev communities. 1,000+ stars is the credibility floor.                                                                                                  |
| **Internal Champions**   | Target company employees encounter and use the work | Targeted presence where specific companies' engineers spend time. Contributions to their open source. Engagement with their engineering blogs. Content that addresses problems they've publicly documented. |

### The Litmus Test

Before any communications investment, ask: _Which of the three value channels does this serve? If none, why are we doing it?_

Not every activity must serve a channel directly. Some activities maintain relationships, build goodwill, or sustain the founders' energy. But the default expectation is that communications work serves the acquihire, and deviations are conscious choices, not drift.

## 3. Three Divisions

The communications operation has three structurally distinct divisions. They differ in medium, privacy posture, agent staffing, cadence, and production model. Cross-division coordination happens at the strategy level, not the production level.

### 1:1 — Relationship Management

**What it is:** Functionally sales. A CRM-shaped operation managing high-value individual relationships.

**Operating model:** Contact list categorized by objective. Notes on all communications. Health tracking. Inbound triage. Meeting selectivity criteria.

**Current state:** Shaped heavily by inbound — who shows up. This is fine at early stage, but requires a framework for saying no to low-value meetings and being more intentional about who gets direct time.

**Key question:** Who are the right 50 people for the acquihire? This is the O1 tier from the Orbit Model — not the biggest audience, but the highest-gravity relationships.

**Relationship tiers:**

- **Build Urgently (O1)** — The right 50. Highest-value relationships for the acquihire. Engineering leaders, product executives, and internal champions at target companies.
- **Build (O2)** — Worth investing in. Emerging relationships with potential. People who could become O1 with the right interaction.
- **Maintain (O3-O4)** — Existing relationships that need upkeep, not active investment. Locals, general network, acquaintances.

**Privacy posture:** Highest. No contact data, relationship notes, or conversation content is shared beyond the division. 1:1 intelligence never bleeds into 1:Many content.

**Acquihire service:** Primarily Channel 1 (thought leadership with the right 50) and Channel 3 (internal champions). The relationship is the delivery mechanism.

### Groups — Community Leadership

**What it is:** Facilitation, events, shared strategic documents. Lower volume, higher complexity per interaction. Different privacy context than the other divisions.

**Operating model:** Purpose-first design (Priya Parker). Every group starts with: "What is this group trying to solve?" Facilitation cadences, event planning, shared document conventions, group health monitoring.

**Current state:** The Silvering Chalices men's group is the active example. Complex political dynamics, facilitation challenges, leadership energy management. The AI-assisted strategic document for this group was the catalyst for the entire communications factory vision.

**Privacy posture:** Group-level. Intelligence is shared within the group context but never with 1:Many. What happens in a group stays in the group unless explicitly approved for sharing.

**Acquihire service:** Primarily Channel 1 (thought leadership). Community leadership is a credibility signal — someone who can facilitate complex group dynamics is someone who can lead teams.

### 1:Many — Content & Thought Leadership

**What it is:** The "marketing factory." Blog posts, video scripts, newsletters, social media, contests. Production pipeline with content backlog and editorial calendar. Highest volume, most systematizable.

**Operating model:** Content factory mirroring the software factory: Decide > Shape > Make > Patch. Content backlog (pull system, not fixed calendar). Appetite + circuit breaker for every piece. Betting table for allocation decisions.

**Current state:** Almost nothing. Barely anything is happening — which is up from nothing. The little that exists has generated motion and established provenance, but it cannot scale without the strategy and systems being built here.

**Privacy posture:** Public. Produced for external audiences. Everything in 1:Many is designed to be seen.

**Acquihire service:** All three channels. 1:Many is the broadest surface area for the acquihire strategy. Thought leadership content reaches the right 50. Open source documentation and community engagement drive traction. Content addressing target companies' problems creates the conditions for internal champions.

## 4. PESO Weighting

Using Gini Dietrich's PESO framework, weighted for an acquihire strategy:

| Media Type | Weight    | What It Means for Us                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| ---------- | --------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Owned**  | Primary   | Blog, demo video, open source documentation, context library. Assets we control, that persist, that an acquirer's corp dev team finds when they check us out. The foundation.                           |
| **Shared** | Secondary | LinkedIn (first channel), X/Twitter, dev communities, GitHub discussions. Where the right 50 actually are. Algorithms and community behavior drive reach, but the content originates from owned assets. |
| **Earned** | Tertiary  | Conference mentions, podcast guest spots, community recognition. Valuable but not actively pursued — let it emerge from owned + shared quality.                                                         |
| **Paid**   | Minimal   | Not the right play for acquihire. If used at all, only to amplify specific high-performing owned content to a precisely targeted audience.                                                              |

**Implication:** The majority of communications investment goes into owned assets. Shared channels distribute owned content. Earned media is a lagging indicator of quality, not a leading activity.

## 6. Content Pillars

Four repeating themes that anchor all content. Every piece of content maps to a pillar. Pillars ensure thematic coherence without prescribing specific topics.

**Pillar 1: Living in the Future**
We live and work in the thing we're building. Not theoretically — literally. Our projects are hex tiles on a spatial map. Our AI teammates have defined jobs. Our weeks have structure and rhythm. This pillar is the invitation: _come see what it looks like._ Every piece of content here is a tour — a screenshot of the Life Map, a walkthrough of a planning session, a moment where the spatial interface revealed something a list never could.

- Serves: Thought Leadership (primary), Internal Champions
- Content types: Product walkthroughs, spatial screenshots, "a day in LifeBuild," demo clips, the factory room video, before/after comparisons of spatial vs. list-based work
- Unique angle: No one else can show this because no one else has built it. You cannot fake a traversable future.
- This is where both crown jewel assets live: the product demo video (tour the product) and the factory room video (tour the factory).

**Pillar 2: The Factory Model**
How a 2-person team ships like a 10-person team. The agent architecture, the context library as infrastructure, the production pipeline. Still "build in public" — but now in service of Pillar 1. The factory is _how_ we built the future you're touring.

- Serves: Thought Leadership, Internal Champions
- Content types: Process walkthroughs, production metrics, decision logs, agent coordination examples
- Unique angle: No one else is documenting an AI-native production operation from the inside at this level of specificity

**Pillar 3: The Context Library**
Institutional knowledge for AI. How the context library works, why it matters, what it solves. This is the primary pillar for open source traction — every piece of content here drives people toward the GitHub repo.

- Serves: Open Source Traction, Internal Champions
- Content types: Technical deep dives, usage guides, case studies, contribution guides
- Unique angle: Solves a problem every serious dev tools company faces and most haven't solved

**Pillar 4: Human-AI Teaming**
What working alongside AI teammates actually looks like — not in theory, but in daily practice. The relationship dynamics, the judgment calls, the surprises. Connected to Pillar 1: the spatial interface is _where_ teaming happens. You can see your agents on the map. They have positions. The relationship is visible and traversable, not hidden behind a chat interface.

- Serves: Thought Leadership, Internal Champions
- Content types: Personal narratives, lessons learned, workflow revelations, failed experiments, agent interaction vignettes
- Unique angle: Lived experience from people doing the work, not studying it. The spatial dimension adds something no one else has — teaming you can _see_.

### Pillar Relationships

Pillar 1 (Living in the Future) is the umbrella. Pillars 2-4 are the supporting structure:

```
Living in the Future (the invitation)
    |
    +-- The Factory Model (how we built this future)
    +-- The Context Library (the infrastructure under it)
    +-- Human-AI Teaming (what it's like to live here)
```

Every piece of content from Pillars 2-4 can be framed as a deeper dive into something visible from Pillar 1. A factory model post is "here's how the sausage gets made in the world you just toured." A context library post is "here's the foundation that makes the world possible." A human-AI teaming post is "here's what it feels like to live here."

## 8. Operating Model

### Content Backlog (Pull System)

No rigid editorial calendar. Ideas enter a prioritized backlog from multiple sources: daily capture, founder conversations, user feedback, factory observations, market signals. Content is pulled into production when capacity allows.

The editorial calendar exists for coordination and visibility — what's in flight, what's coming — but it is not a commitment device. A 2-person team cannot commit to "publish every Tuesday." It can commit to "pull the next best thing from the backlog when we have capacity."

### Appetite + Circuit Breaker

Every content piece gets a time budget (appetite) before production begins:

- LinkedIn post: 1-2 hours
- LinkedIn thread: half day
- Blog post: 1-2 days
- Video: 1 week
- Newsletter issue: half day

If a piece can't be completed within its appetite, it's killed or reshaped into a smaller container. A blog post that's been in MAKE for three weeks becomes a LinkedIn thread — same insight, smaller container. No extensions. This prevents the graveyard of 80%-done content.

### Betting Table

Periodically (biweekly or as backlog accumulates), review shaped content ideas and decide which get appetite. Not everything shaped gets built. The betting table is where resource allocation happens at the tactical level.

Criteria for betting:

1. Which value channel does it serve?
2. Does it map to a content pillar?
3. What's the channel-market fit? (Will it reach the right people?)
4. What's the appetite? (Can we afford it right now?)
5. Is there a timeliness factor? (Will it be less relevant next week?)

### Weekly Review (15 Minutes)

Start of each week. Track against the three value channels + division health:

| Check           | What to Look At                                                                 |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Value Channel 1 | Who saw our content this week? Any engagement from the right 50?                |
| Value Channel 2 | GitHub stars, forks, external contributors, organic discovery?                  |
| Value Channel 3 | Any engagement from target company employees? Relevant job postings at targets? |
| 1:1 Health      | Meaningful interactions? Relationship health changes? Inbound quality?          |
| Groups Health   | Any events? Group dynamics? Political situations?                               |
| 1:Many Health   | What shipped? What's in pipeline? Content performance?                          |
| Pipeline        | What's in DECIDE, SHAPE, MAKE, PATCH? Anything past its appetite?               |

### Human Approval Gate

No AI-drafted communications are dispatched without human review and approval. AI produces; human dispatches. This applies to all three divisions, all channels, all content types. The line is clear: agents draft, research, strategize, and produce. Humans decide, approve, and send.

## 9. Sequencing

### What to Build First

1. **This document** (done) — the strategic frame
2. **Content pillars** — the thematic anchors (requires human approval of proposed pillars)
3. **The Right 50 list** — who are we trying to reach? (O1 identification)
4. **LinkedIn channel strategy** — the first channel, operationalized
5. **Content backlog** — seed it with 10-20 ideas mapped to pillars
6. **First content** — pull the highest-value item and produce it

### What to Defer

- Newsletter, blog, video channel strategies — until LinkedIn is proven
- Playbooks — until the divisions are documented and operational
- Detailed editorial calendar — until the backlog exists and we have cadence data
- CRM tooling decisions — until the right 50 are identified and the relationship model is clear
- Group expansion — until the current group (Silvering Chalices) is stable

### The Principle: Strategy Before Factory

The biggest problem identified in the socialization vision: no master strategy exists. The factory should not be built before the strategy layer exists. This document is the strategy layer. Now the factory can be built — but in sequence, not all at once.

### Lagging Indicators (Monthly/Quarterly)

| Signal                             | What It Means                                                                          |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Right user retention               | VCs, founders, or exec-level operators still using LifeBuild 90+ days after onboarding |
| Unprompted advocacy                | An alpha user shares LifeBuild with someone we don't know, without being asked         |
| Target company employee engagement | Anyone at a priority acquirer target is a current user or engages with content         |
| Demo video circulation             | The demo is shared by someone with pull without a warm introduction                    |
| "I've been trying to build this"   | A product leader at a target says "we've been thinking about this problem"             |
| GitHub stars velocity              | Trending toward 1,000+ organically                                                     |

### The Anti-Metric

Do not track: follower count, impression count, or any metric that measures audience size without audience precision. Ten thousand impressions from the wrong people is worth less than ten impressions from the right people.

## 12. Key Risks

| Risk                                         | Mitigation                                                                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Spreading too thin**                       | One channel first. Appetite + circuit breaker on every piece. Say no to everything that doesn't serve a value channel.                      |
| **Philosophizing instead of showing**        | Content pillar discipline. "Living in the Future" pillar is about showing the world we inhabit, not speculating about what AI might become. |
| **Reactive 1:1 consuming all time**          | Inbound triage playbook. Framework for saying no. Right 50 list as a decision filter.                                                       |
| **Building the factory before the strategy** | This document. Strategy before factory. The principle is now documented.                                                                    |
| **Losing energy**                            | Cool-down periods between cycles. Circuit breakers prevent the 80%-done content graveyard. Celebrate what shipped, not what's planned.      |
| **Wrong 50 people**                          | Regular review of the O1 list. The list is a living document, not a fixed target. Someone can move from O2 to O1 or drop off entirely.      |

## Summary

This is not a marketing strategy. This is an acquihire visibility strategy built on a single premise: _we are living in the future of work, and you can come see it._

The strategy is executed through three divisions (1:1, Groups, 1:Many) across cross-cutting channels, anchored by four content pillars (Living in the Future, The Factory Model, The Context Library, Human-AI Teaming), governed by a pull-based production system with appetite budgets and circuit breakers, and measured by signals that track whether the right people are encountering the work.

Two crown jewel content bets — the product demo video (tour the product) and the factory room video (tour the factory) — sit above the regular content backlog as strategic investments.

The single highest-leverage action right now: identify the right 50 people, start publishing on LinkedIn 1-2x per week from the Living in the Future pillar, and begin planning the product demo video.

Everything else is sequence and system. This document is the frame. Now we build the walls.
